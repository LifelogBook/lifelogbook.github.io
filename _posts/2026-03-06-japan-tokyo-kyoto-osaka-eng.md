> This is not just a tourist guide — it's a pre-trip study note on the historical context behind each location.
> Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka each have a distinct identity: the **Shogun's capital**, the **Emperor's capital**, and the **Merchant's capital**.

---

## Japan's Historical Timeline at a Glance

```
645    Taika Reform → Attempt to establish an Emperor-centered state
794    Capital moved to Kyoto (Heiankyō) → Aristocratic culture flourishes
1185   Kamakura Shogunate → Shōgun holds real power; age of the samurai begins
1336   Muromachi Shogunate → Shōgun resides in Kyoto; Kinkakuji & Ginkakuji built
1467   Ōnin War → Sengoku (Warring States) period begins
1590   Toyotomi Hideyoshi unifies Japan; uses Osaka Castle as his base
1600   Battle of Sekigahara → Tokugawa Ieyasu victorious
1603   Edo Shogunate → Shōgun in Tokyo (Edo), Emperor in Kyoto; Nijō Castle built to monitor the court
1868   Meiji Restoration → Emperor moves to Tokyo; Westernization & modernization
Today  Tokyo (politics & economy), Kyoto (ancient capital & culture), Osaka (economy & popular culture)
```

# Part 1 — Tokyo (東京)

> Tokyo = Edo (江戸): Japan's de facto center since the Tokugawa Shogunate in 1603; official capital after the Emperor's move in 1869

---

## 1. Tokyo Station & the Imperial Palace (皇居)

### Tokyo Station
- Opened in 1914, designed by architect Tatsuno Kingo (辰野金吾)
- The red-brick building is a symbol of the Meiji era's Westernization policy
- Partially destroyed in WWII air raids → restored to original form in 2012

### Imperial Palace (皇居) — Residence of the Emperor
- Originally **Edo Castle (江戸城)**: the stronghold of the Tokugawa Shogunate (1603–1868)
  - After Tokugawa Ieyasu's victory at the Battle of Sekigahara (1600) and unification of Japan
  - For 265 years, the Shōgun held real power while the Emperor was effectively confined in Kyoto
- After the **Meiji Restoration (1868)**, the Emperor moved from Kyoto to Tokyo → Edo Castle was converted into the Imperial Palace
  - The feudal domain system was dismantled through the return of domains (hanseki hōkan) and abolition of domains (haihan chiken)
  - A centralized state under the Emperor was established
- Currently the residence of the Imperial Family; only certain areas open to the public (e.g., Jan. 1 and Feb. 23)

### Meganebashi (眼鏡橋)
- The stone bridge in front of the Nijūbashi (二重橋) at the Imperial Palace
- Two arches reflected in the water resemble a pair of glasses, hence the nickname "Glasses Bridge"
- A product of Western civil engineering techniques introduced during the Meiji era

## 2. Rengatei & the History of Meat-Eating in Japan (煉瓦亭)

### 1,200 Years of the Meat-Eating Ban
- **675 AD, Emperor Tenmu** issued a prohibition on eating meat: killing cattle, horses, dogs, monkeys, and chickens was banned
  - Rooted in the Buddhist prohibition on taking life + the role of cattle and horses as agricultural labor
- Subsequent emperors repeatedly reinforced the ban → officially observed for approximately **1,200 years** through the Edo period
- However, it was never absolute: wild game such as boar and deer, consumed as "medicinal food" (yakushoku, 薬食), was tacitly permitted

### The Meiji Restoration and the Lifting of the Meat Ban
- **1872, Emperor Meiji** reportedly ate beef himself, as reported in newspapers → official end of the ban
- The idea spread that "Westerners are strong because they eat meat" — part of the civilization and enlightenment (bunmei kaika) movement
- **Gyūnabe (牛鍋)**: the first widely popular beef dish — beef braised with soy sauce, sugar, and tofu in a hot pot
  - The origin of today's sukiyaki (すき焼き)

### Pork and Tempura → The Birth of Tonkatsu
- Pork was initially prepared in the **tempura (天ぷら)** style (thin batter coating)
- **Pork Cutlet** → Japanese pronunciation **pōku katsuretsu (ポークカツレツ)** → **Tonkatsu (とんかつ)**
- **Rengatei (煉瓦亭, est. 1895)**: a Western-style restaurant in Ginza
  - Thick panko (パン粉) breading + steamed rice, miso soup, and shredded cabbage → Japanese-style tonkatsu perfected
  - The origin of **yōshoku (洋食)** culture: Western dishes reimagined as Japanese set meals

### Western Foods Japanized (洋食·和洋折衷)

| Original | Japanese Version | Key Transformation |
|----------|------------------|--------------------|
| Indian curry (via Britain) | **Curry Rice (カレーライス)** | Thick roux-based sauce, served with rice |
| European bread (no filling) | **Anpan (あんぱん)** | Created in 1874 by Kimuraya; filled with sweet red bean paste |
| French Croquette | **Korokke (コロッケ)** | Luxury dish → simplified with potato & ground meat; became a common side dish |
| Pork Cutlet | **Tonkatsu** | Thick meat, panko breading, shredded cabbage |

*Rengatei is located in Ginza (about 10 min walk from Tokyo Station); lunch available without a reservation — just queue up*

## 3. Ueno Park (上野公園)

### Edo Period — The Grounds of Kan'eiji Temple
- Ueno Hill was the temple precinct of **Kan'eiji (寛永寺)**, established by the Edo Shogunate
- A family temple (bodaiji) of the Tokugawa clan, with tombs of many Shōguns
- Served as the guardian of Edo's "demon gate" (kimon, the inauspicious northeast direction) → a spiritual sanctuary of the Shogunate

### The Boshin War — The Last Clash Between the Shogunate and the Meiji Government (1868)
- After the Meiji Restoration, imperial forces (centered on Satsuma and Chōshū domains) vs. pro-Shogunate **Shōgitai (彰義隊)**
- **Battle of Ueno (上野戦争, May 1868)**: The Shōgitai gathered at Kan'eiji
  - Imperial forces bombarded them with Western-style Armstrong cannons → crushed in a single day
  - Most of Kan'eiji was destroyed; the Shōgitai collapsed
- This battle ended all Shogunate resistance within Edo (Tokyo)
- Subsequently opened as a public park (designated in 1873 as one of Japan's first public parks)

### Today
- Famous for cherry blossoms; home to the Tokyo National Museum, Tokyo Metropolitan Art Museum, National Museum of Western Art, and more
- Shinobazu Pond (不忍池): a celebrated scenic spot since the Edo period

---

## 4. Tokyo National Museum (東京国立博物館)

- Founded in 1872, Japan's oldest and largest museum
- Located in Ueno Park; operates a total of 5 buildings

### Viewing Strategy — Short on Time? Head to **2nd Floor of the Honkan (Japanese Gallery)**
- 2nd floor of the Honkan: a chronological overview of Japanese art history in concentrated form
  - Jōmon pottery → Yayoi bronzeware → Buddhist art → Samurai culture (swords & armor) → Edo-era painting (ukiyo-e)
- Tōyōkan (Eastern Gallery): Art from China, Korea, and Southeast Asia (including Korean gallery)
- Heiseikan: Special exhibitions & permanent archaeology gallery

---

## 5. The University of Tokyo & Akamon (東京大学·赤門)

### Akamon (赤門) — The Gate of the Maeda Clan
- **Maeda Clan (前田家)**: Lords of the Kaga Domain (加賀藩) in the Edo period; the largest domain after the Tokugawa
  - Kaga Hyakumangoku (百万石) — Japan's largest outer domain, measured by rice production capacity
- **1827**: Built when the 11th lord Maeda Nariyasu wed a daughter from the Shōgun's family
  - A gate for a woman of the Shōgun's lineage (goshuden-mon, 御守殿門) → painting it red was a customary requirement
- After the Meiji Restoration, the Maeda estate was taken over by the state

### The Birth of the University of Tokyo
- **1877**: Merger of Tokyo Kaisei School and Tokyo Medical School → **University of Tokyo** founded (Japan's first modern national university)
- **1886**: Renamed **Imperial University (帝國大學)** under the Imperial University Ordinance → became the institution for training Japan's elite bureaucrats
- Built on the former Maeda estate grounds, Akamon naturally became the main gate
- Akamon is a designated Important Cultural Property; the Yasuda Auditorium (1925) on the Hongō campus is also notable historic architecture

## Tokyo Historical Summary

```
Edo Period (1603–1868)
 ├─ Tokyo = Edo: capital of the Shogunate
 ├─ Site of the Imperial Palace = Edo Castle (Shōgun's residence)
 ├─ Ueno = Kan'eiji, guardian temple of the Shogunate
 ├─ Maeda Estate = present-day University of Tokyo campus
 └─ Meat-eating ban in effect

Meiji Restoration (1868–)
 ├─ Battle of Ueno crushes the Shogunate's last resistance
 ├─ Emperor moves from Kyoto to Tokyo; Edo Castle → Imperial Palace
 ├─ Lifting of the meat ban → gyūnabe, tonkatsu, and yōshoku cuisine born
 ├─ University of Tokyo founded (1877), Tokyo Station built (1914)
 └─ Westernization Japanized (curry rice, korokke, anpan)
```

# Part 2 — Kyoto (京都)

> Kyoto = Heiankyō (平安京): Japan's capital from 794 to 1869 — approximately 1,075 years

---

## The Big Picture for Understanding Kyoto

```
794    Capital moved to Heiankyō → Kyoto becomes the capital; Emperor resides here
1185   Kamakura Shogunate established → Shōgun holds real power; Emperor remains in Kyoto
1336   Muromachi Shogunate → Shōgun resides in Kyoto (Kinkakuji & Ginkakuji built)
1467   Ōnin War → Most of Kyoto destroyed; Sengoku period begins
1603   Edo Shogunate → Shōgun in Edo; Emperor in Kyoto (Nijō Castle built)
1869   Meiji Restoration → Emperor moves to Tokyo; Kyoto becomes an ancient capital
```

## 1. Tōji Five-Story Pagoda (東寺 五重塔)

### The Construction of Heiankyō and the Founding of Tōji & Saiji
- **794, Emperor Kanmu** moved the capital from Nara to Heiankyō
  - A problem of the Nara period: Buddhist institutions had too much political influence → moving the capital aimed at separating religion from politics
  - Heiankyō was modeled after Chang'an, the Tang dynasty capital — a grid-patterned city
- **Tōji (東寺)** and **Saiji (西寺)** were built on either side of **Rajōmon (羅城門)**, the main southern gate of the capital
  - A pair of state-sponsored temples for the protection of the nation
  - **Saiji was destroyed by fire and financial ruin in the late Heian period**, leaving no trace today
  - Only Tōji survived, standing for over 1,200 years

### Kūkai (空海) and Tōji
- **823, Emperor Saga** gifted Tōji to **Kūkai (空海, 774–835)**
- Kūkai studied Esoteric Buddhism (Shingon) in Tang China and returned to Japan as the founder of Shingon Buddhism
- Developed Tōji into the head temple of the Shingon school

### The Five-Story Pagoda
- The tallest surviving wooden pagoda in Japan, standing approximately **55 meters**
- Repeatedly destroyed by lightning and fire, then rebuilt; the current pagoda was reconstructed in **1644** (with patronage from Tokugawa Iemitsu)
- UNESCO World Heritage Site (inscribed 1994)

---

## 2. Kiyomizudera (清水寺)

### Founding and History
- **778**: Monk Enchin (延鎭) founded the temple after receiving a vision of Kannon Bodhisattva at Otowa Falls (legendary)
- **798**: Warrior Sakanoue no Tamuramaro (坂上田村麻呂), while hunting deer, came upon the site and converted; he built a large temple complex
- The current buildings were rebuilt in **1633 by Tokugawa Iemitsu** (wooden construction using no nails)
- UNESCO World Heritage Site

### Kiyomizuzaka (清水坂)
- The approach path up to Kiyomizudera, a shopping street that developed from the Edo period
- Concentrated with ceramics, lacquerware, and Kyoto confectionery (wagashi) shops → center of Kiyomizuyaki (清水焼) Kyoto ceramics
- Connected to Sannenzaka (三年坂) and Ninenzaka (二年坂)

### Key Points in the Precincts
- **Niōmon (仁王門, Deva Gate)**: Red-lacquered gate with muscular Niō (Kongō Rikishi) guardian statues on each side
  - The pair of statues represent *a* (阿) and *un* (吽) — symbolizing the beginning and end of the universe
- **Three-Story Pagoda (三重塔)**: About 31m tall, one of the largest three-story pagodas in Japan; interior enshrines the Eight Patriarchs of Shingon
- **Hondō Butai (本堂舞台, the Main Hall Stage)**:
  - Supported by 139 zelkova (keyaki) pillars on a 13-meter cliff
  - "To jump off the Kiyomizudera stage" — a Japanese idiom for making a bold, courageous decision
  - Historical records from the Edo period: 234 people actually jumped; approximately 85% survived (the belief was that surviving granted one's wish)

### Otowa Falls (音羽の滝) — Three Streams
- The three streams symbolize: ① academic success & wisdom ② love & good relationships ③ longevity & health
- Custom: **drink from only one** — legend holds that wishing for all three cancels out any benefit
- The temple's name *Kiyomizudera* (清水寺) = "Temple of Pure Water," derived from these falls

---

## 3. Ninenzaka & Sannenzaka (二年坂·三年坂)

- Stone-paved approach paths connecting Kiyomizudera to Yasaka Shrine and Gion
- **Ninenzaka**: Legend says "if you stumble here, misfortune will come within 2 years"
- **Sannenzaka**: "If you stumble here, misfortune will come within 3 years"
  - In reality, the stories arose as warnings not to get hurt on the slippery stone steps
- Well-preserved lattice-windowed wooden machiya (町家, merchant townhouses) from the Edo–Meiji periods line both sides
- A **historic townscape preservation district** unique to Kyoto, linking Gion and Kiyomizudera

---

## 4. Gion (祇園)

### Historical Formation
- **Monzenmachi (門前町)** of Yasaka Shrine: naturally developed as a district of lodging, food, and entertainment for shrine visitors
- Grew into an officially licensed entertainment district (hanamachi, 花街) during the Edo period
- **Hanamikoji (花見小路)**: Gion's main street, lined on both sides with ochaya (御茶屋, high-end teahouses / dining establishments)
  - Ochaya operate on a closed, introduction-only basis — entry without a reservation or introduction is not permitted

### Maiko and Geiko Culture
- **Maiko (舞妓)**: Apprentice geigi (performing artists), typically girls aged 15–20. Known for elaborate flower hairpins, long obi sashes, and white makeup
- **Geiko (芸妓)**: Adult performing artists trained in dance, shamisen, tea ceremony, and more (known as *geisha* in Tokyo)
- Gion ranks first among Japan's five major hanamachi in both scale and prestige

### Today
- Since the 2020s, overtourism has been an issue → some private alleyways have been closed to tourists (fines for violations)
- You can spot maiko heading to work in the evening–night hours (forced photography and physical contact are strictly prohibited)

---

## 5. Yasaka Shrine (八坂神社)

### Overview
- The central shrine of Gion; principal deity is **Susanoo no Mikoto (素戔嗚尊)**
  - Susanoo: the storm god of Japanese mythology; younger brother of Amaterasu (the sun goddess)
- Founding legend: **656 AD**, a Goguryeo envoy brought a deity from the Korean Peninsula (one academic theory)
- Official founding records: origins in a ritual held in 869 to ward off plague

### Gion Matsuri (祇園祭)
- One of **Japan's Three Great Festivals** (alongside Kanda Matsuri in Tokyo and Tenjin Matsuri in Osaka)
- Held throughout the **entire month of July** each year; highlights are the **Yamaboko Junkō (山鉾巡行)** processions on July 17 and 24
  - Yama (山) and Hoko (鉾): elaborately decorated floats reaching up to 25 meters in height
  - Floats built in the 14th–16th centuries are decorated with Persian and Indian textiles → reflecting the international trade of the era
- Origin: a nationwide plague in 869 → prayers at Yasaka Shrine; the festival has continued for **over 1,150 years**

## 6. Sanjūsangendō (三十三間堂)

### The Building
- Official name: **Rengeouin (蓮華王院)**
- Built in **1165** at the behest of Retired Emperor Go-Shirakawa (後白河上皇)
- **33 ken (間)**: the number of bays between pillars = 33; total length approximately **120 meters**
  - The longest wooden building in Japan
  - 33 is derived from the Buddhist teaching that Kannon Bodhisattva can transform into 33 different forms

### 1,001 Senju Kannon Statues (千手観音像)
- In the center: **1 seated statue** (3.36m tall, National Treasure) + **1,000 standing statues** on each side = **1,001 total**
- Features of each statue:
  - **11 faces (面)**: 10 small faces above the main face + 1 main face
  - **40 arms (手)**: 40 additional arms beyond the two main ones
  - **40 arms × 25 wishes = 1,000 wishes** can be granted — a Buddhist calculation
- Legend has it that among the 1,000 figures, there is one that resembles the face of your family member or loved one
- In the Edo period, an archery contest called **Tōshiya (通し矢)** was held under the eaves of the main hall → still reenacted every January

## 7. Kinkakuji (金閣寺 / 鹿苑寺)

### The Muromachi Shogunate and Ashikaga Yoshimitsu
- **1336, Ashikaga Takauji** founded the Muromachi Shogunate
- **3rd Shōgun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu (足利義満)**: the golden age of the Muromachi Shogunate
  - Unification of the Northern and Southern Courts (1392): reunified two rival imperial lineages that had been divided for 60 years
  - **Nichi-Min Trade (日明貿易)**: Official trade with Ming Dynasty China; accumulated enormous wealth
  - Yoshimitsu himself received investiture from the Ming Emperor as "King of Japan" (not the Emperor, but the Shōgun claimed the royal title)
- **1397**: Built a villa at Kitayama (北山) → the present-day Kinkakuji

### The Three-Story Structure — A Blend of Three Architectural Styles

| Floor | Style | Significance |
|-------|-------|--------------|
| 1st floor (Hōsuiin) | Heian aristocratic architecture (Shinden-zukuri) | Respect for aristocratic culture |
| 2nd floor (Chōonkaku) | Kamakura samurai architecture (Buke-zukuri) | Symbol of warrior rule |
| 3rd floor (Kukkyōchō) | Zen Buddhist temple architecture (Zenshū Butsuden-zukuri) | Buddhist ideal |

- Exterior walls of the 2nd and 3rd floors are covered in **gold leaf** → a display of wealth accumulated through international trade
- Garden: a pond garden designed to represent **Jōdo (Pure Land paradise)**, with Kinkakuji reflected in Kyōkochi (鏡湖池, Mirror Pond)

### Destruction and Reconstruction
- **1950**: Apprentice Rinzai monk Hayashi Shōken (age 22) committed arson → the temple was completely burned down
  - Motive: mental anguish caused by an "obsession with the beauty" of Kinkakuji
  - **Mishima Yukio (三島由紀夫)** wrote the novel *The Temple of the Golden Pavilion (金閣寺, 1956)* based on this incident → a masterpiece of modern Japanese literature
- **1955**: Rebuilt as the current Kinkakuji

---

## 8. Ginkakuji (銀閣寺 / 慈照寺)

### Ashikaga Yoshimasa and the Ōnin War
- **8th Shōgun Ashikaga Yoshimasa (足利義政)**: a period of Shogunate decline
- **Ōnin War (応仁の乱, 1467–1477)**: An internal conflict that began over the question of Shogunal succession
  - Lasted 11 years with Kyoto as the battlefield → most of the city was destroyed
  - The direct trigger for the Sengoku (Warring States) period
  - While war raged, Yoshimasa devoted himself to the arts and tea ceremony → simultaneously a symbol of incompetent leadership and a patron of culture

### Construction of Ginkakuji (1482)
- After the war, a villa was built on the Higashiyama (東山) hillside
- It is widely believed that gold leaf was planned but never applied due to financial difficulties
  - Alternative theory: the sand garden glittered silver in moonlight, leading to the name "Silver Pavilion"
- Fewer tourists than Kinkakuji → **a quieter, more contemplative atmosphere**

### Higashiyama Culture (東山文化) and Japanese Aesthetics
- Paradoxically, the chaos of the Ōnin War gave birth to **uniquely Japanese aesthetics**
- **Wabi (侘び)**: finding beauty in simplicity and imperfection
- **Sabi (寂び)**: finding beauty in the passage of time and quiet solitude
- Products of Higashiyama Culture:
  - **Tea Ceremony (茶道)**: initiated by Murata Jukō → later perfected by Sen no Rikyū
  - **Nō (能)**: masked music-drama perfected by Zeami (世阿弥)
  - **Kadō (花道)**: the art of flower arrangement (ikebana)
  - **Ink Painting (水墨画)**: Zen monk-painters such as Sesshū (雪舟)
- **Ginshanada (銀沙灘)**: a garden sculpture of sand raked into wave patterns, designed to reflect moonlight

## 9. Kyoto Imperial Palace (京都御所)

### The Capital of Kyoto and the Emperor
- The Emperor's residence from the **794** move to Heiankyō until Emperor Meiji's move to Tokyo in **1869** — approximately **1,075 years**
- The current buildings were rebuilt after a fire in **1855** (in the late Edo style)

### The Emperor's Actual Power
- Throughout the Kyoto era, direct imperial rule was rare
  - Late Heian: **Fujiwara Clan Regency (摂関政治)** — maternal relatives held real power
  - Insei (院政): Retired Emperors held power; the reigning Emperor was a symbolic figure
  - Kamakura–Edo: Shōguns held power; Emperors were effectively confined in Kyoto
- Yet the Emperor remained the **source of legitimacy** — even Shōguns required Imperial appointment (*Sei-i Taishōgun*)

---

## 10. Nijō Castle (二条城)

### Background — The Shogunate's Strategic Foothold in Kyoto
- Built in **1603 by Tokugawa Ieyasu** to commemorate his appointment as Sei-i Taishōgun after the Battle of Sekigahara
- Purposes:
  1. **Residence during visits to Kyoto**: a lodging for the Shōgun, who was based in Edo
  2. **Monitoring the Emperor**: located right next to the Imperial Palace (Gosho) → kept watch on the Imperial court
  3. **Demonstration of power**: a declaration of Shogunate authority to the Kyoto nobility and court

### Architectural Features
- **Ninomaru Palace (二の丸御殿)**: A National Treasure and the finest surviving example of samurai residential architecture
  - Decorated with gilded folding screens by painters of the Kanō School (a total of 3,600 works)
- **Nightingale Floors (鶯張り, Uguisubari)**: The floorboards squeak like bird calls when walked on → a security device to detect intruders

### Historic Moments
- **1603**: Start of the Edo Shogunate; Tokugawa Ieyasu proclaimed his authority here
- **October 15, 1867**: **Taisei Hōkan (大政奉還, Return of Political Power to the Emperor)**
  - The 15th Shōgun Tokugawa Yoshinobu announced the return of 265 years of Shogunate power to the Emperor in the Great Reception Hall of Nijō Castle
  - Nijō Castle witnessed both the beginning (1603) and the end (1867) of the Edo Shogunate

---

## 11. Arashiyama (嵐山)

### Togetsukyō Bridge (渡月橋)
- "The bridge the moon crosses" — Heian aristocrats, watching from boats, named it after the moon appearing to cross the bridge
- A beloved spot for Heian aristocrats to enjoy outings and moon-viewing; over 1,000 years of history
- The current bridge was rebuilt in 1934, stretching approximately 155 meters over the Katsura River (桂川)

### Bamboo Grove (竹林の道)
- A roughly 400-meter path of bamboo from Tenryūji to Nonomiya Shrine
- Dense rows of mōsō bamboo 10–20 meters tall on both sides
- The rustling sound in the breeze → selected by Japan's Ministry of the Environment as one of the "100 Soundscapes of Japan We Want to Preserve"

### Tenryūji (天龍寺)
- Founded in **1339 by Ashikaga Takauji** (founding Shōgun of the Muromachi Shogunate)
  - Built to appease the spirit of Emperor Go-Daigo (後醍醐天皇)
- Garden **Sōgenchi (曹源池)**: designed by Musō Soseki (夢窓疎石); the first garden in Japan simultaneously designated a Historic Site and Special Scenic Spot
  - A stroll-style garden centered on a pond; uses the Arashiyama mountains as a borrowed landscape (shakkei)
- UNESCO World Heritage Site

### Nonomiya Shrine (野宮神社)
- Site of purification rituals for the **Saio (斎王)** of Ise Grand Shrine — in the Heian period, an imperial princess would undergo purification here before departing for Ise
- A setting in **The Tale of Genji (源氏物語)**: the scene where Hikaru Genji bids farewell to Lady Rokujō
- Famous for love, marriage, and academic success; known for its characteristic black-wood torii gate (kuroki torii, 黒木鳥居)

# Part 3 — Osaka (大阪)

> Osaka = Naniwa (難波): from ancient times, a gateway for trade via the Seto Inland Sea (瀬戸内海); the economic heart of Japan during the Edo period

---

## The Big Picture for Understanding Osaka

```
645    Emperor Kōtoku establishes a capital (Naniwa Palace) in Naniwa → Osaka's first capital
794    Capital moved to Heiankyō → Kyoto becomes capital; Naniwa continues as a trading port city
1583   Toyotomi Hideyoshi builds Osaka Castle → base for national unification
1615   Tokugawa wins the Summer Siege of Osaka → Toyotomi clan destroyed; Osaka comes under Tokugawa rule
       → Edo period: thrives as the "Kitchen of the Nation" — an economic city
```

## 1. Osaka Castle (大阪城)

### Three Heroes of the Sengoku Era and Osaka

**Oda Nobunaga (織田信長, 1534–1582)**
- A daimyō from Aichi, rose to prominence through brilliant tactics and the introduction of firearms (tanegashima)
- Destroyed the Muromachi Shogunate (1573); came one step short of unifying Japan
- 1582 **Honnōji Incident (本能寺の変)**: betrayed by his retainer Akechi Mitsuhide; died by suicide — "the man who reached for the realm but never grasped it"

**Toyotomi Hideyoshi (豊臣秀吉, 1537–1598)**
- Retainer of Nobunaga; of peasant origin, he rose to supreme power (Japan's greatest rags-to-riches story)
- After Nobunaga's death, he defeated Akechi Mitsuhide and completed the unification of Japan (1590)
- **1583**: Began construction of Osaka Castle — at the time Japan's largest fortress, with a golden-decorated keep
  - Built on the site of Ishiyama Honganji (石山本願寺) — a fortress captured after 11 years of war with Nobunaga
- Launched the **Imjin War (1592) and Chongyu War (1597)** — invasions of Korea (Joseon)
- Died in 1598; heir Toyotomi Hideyori was only 5 years old

**Tokugawa Ieyasu (徳川家康, 1543–1616)**
- **Battle of Sekigahara (1600)**: Eastern Army (Tokugawa) vs. Western Army (Toyotomi coalition) — a clash for control of Japan
  - Eastern Army victorious in a single day → the realm effectively decided
- Founded the Edo Shogunate in 1603

### Two Sieges of Osaka (1614–1615) — The Destruction of the Toyotomi Clan

- **Winter Siege of Osaka (1614)**: Tokugawa besieged Osaka Castle → ended in negotiations
  - Negotiated conditions: filling in the moats → Tokugawa secretly filled in even the inner moats (uchibori)
- **Summer Siege of Osaka (1615)**: With its moats filled, Osaka Castle fell
  - Toyotomi Hideyori and his mother Yodogimi died by suicide → the Toyotomi clan was completely extinguished
  - This established 265 years of Tokugawa Shogunate rule

### The Current Osaka Castle
- The current tower keep was rebuilt in **1931** through public fundraising — a reinforced concrete structure (exterior based on Edo-era design; interior is a museum)
- Surrounding **Osakajō Park**: a popular cherry blossom destination

## 2. Osaka Museum of History (大阪歴史博物館)

### Viewing Strategy — Start from the 10th Floor and Work Down
- 10th floor (ancient) → 9th floor (medieval) → 7th floor (Edo-era) → 6th floor (modern, post-Meiji) — arranged in reverse chronological order
- **10th floor**: Diorama reconstruction of Naniwa Palace (難波宮, 7th century) — Osaka's era as an ancient capital
- **7th floor**: Reproduction of the Edo-period "Kitchen of the Nation" merchant city
- Located right next to Osaka Castle

### Osaka = "The Kitchen of the Nation (天下の台所)"

**Geographic Advantage**
- The gateway to the Seto Inland Sea → goods from across western Japan converged in Osaka
- Yodogawa (淀川) river network: inland waterway connecting Osaka to Kyoto
- Development of canal networks such as Dōtonbori → efficient city-wide logistics

**Kurayashiki (蔵屋敷) System**
- Each domain (han) across Japan established a **warehouse-cum-trading post** in Osaka
  - At peak, over 100 domain kurayashiki operated in Osaka
- A national distribution hub for rice, cotton, oil, sake, soy sauce, and more

**Dōjima Rice Market (堂島米市場)**
- Established 1697; **the world's first futures market**
  - Not physical rice but "rice receipt certificates" were traded → the prototype of modern futures trading
- Rice prices controlled the national economy → Osaka merchants effectively controlled the economy of the entire nation

## 3. Dōtonbori (道頓堀)

### History of the Canal
- **1615**: Yasui Dōton (安井道頓) began excavating the canal at his own expense
  - Dōton fought on the Tokugawa side in the Summer Siege of Osaka and died in battle
  - The Tokugawa Shogunate took over the construction and completed it; named the canal "Dōtonbori" in Dōton's honor
- Effect of completing the canal: goods could be shipped directly by boat from Osaka Bay into the heart of the city → explosive commercial growth

### The Dual Character of the Canal's North and South Banks
- **North bank**: From the Edo period, an upscale entertainment district — theaters for Kabuki (歌舞伎) and Ningyō Jōruri (人形浄瑠璃) puppet theater were concentrated here
- **South bank**: Street for commoner food and entertainment → the direct ancestor of today's Dōtonbori dining strip
  - The embodiment of **kuidaore (食い倒れ)** culture — "eating until you drop"

### Today's Icons of Dōtonbori
- **Glico Running Man**: A symbol of Dōtonbori since 1935
  - When the Hanshin Tigers win, fans traditionally jump off Ebisubashi Bridge into the river
- Giant crab sign of Kani Dōraku, the Kuidaore puppet, and other three-dimensional billboard culture — the spirit of Osaka merchants: "stand out and you'll sell"

## 4. Osaka's Three Great Commoner Foods

### Okonomiyaki (お好み焼き)
- Meaning: "Okonomi (as you like it)" + "yaki (grilled)"
- Origins: thin wheat crepes in the Edo period → during WWII food shortages, it spread as a commoner dish made with whatever ingredients were available
- **Osaka-style (Kansai-style)**: ingredients mixed into the batter and grilled thick + mayonnaise, okonomiyaki sauce, katsuobushi (bonito flakes)
- **Hiroshima-style**: ingredients layered and grilled, including soba noodles
- Similar in principle to Korean bindaetteok (mung bean pancake)

### Takoyaki (たこ焼き)
- Meaning: "Tako (octopus)" + "yaki (grilled)"
- **Invented in 1935 in Nishinari, Osaka** by street vendor **Endō Tomikichi (遠藤留吉)**
  - Inspired by the popular meat ball street food → substituted with octopus; exploded in popularity in Osaka, then spread nationwide
- Liquid batter poured into a special hemispherical iron mold + octopus, green onion, ginger → rolled into balls by spinning with chopsticks
- It is said that Osaka households have their own takoyaki iron plates — it is that much a part of daily life

### Kushikatsu (串カツ)
- Meaning: "Kushi (skewer)" + "katsu (cutlet/deep-fried)"
- **Birthplace: Shinsekai (新世界) district of Osaka** — born as cheap drinking food for factory workers
  - Shinsekai: site of the 1903 Domestic Industrial Exposition; later developed into a working-class entertainment district
- **No double-dipping in the sauce (二度漬け禁止)**: the ironclad rule of any kushikatsu restaurant
  - Once you dip into the communal sauce container, that's it — hygiene + shared etiquette

## 5. Tsuruhashi (鶴橋) — Korea Town

### The History of Zainichi Koreans
- **Japanese Colonial Period (1910–1945)**: Forced laborers, mobilized workers, and voluntary migrants converged on Osaka's industrial zone
  - Osaka was an industrial city centered on textiles and manufacturing → explosive demand for low-wage labor
  - Zainichi Koreans formed concentrated residential communities in the Tsuruhashi and Ikuno (生野) areas
- **Post-Liberation (1945–)**: Those unable to return, or who returned and came back, settled permanently
  - Many remained due to the chaos of the GHQ (Allied Occupation) period and the lack of a livelihood base upon returning to Korea
- **Ikuno Ward (生野区)**: The highest proportion of Zainichi Korean residents in Japan (at one point approximately 30% of the population)

### Tsuruhashi Today
- **Tsuruhashi Market**: A labyrinthine alleyway filled with Korean ingredients, kimchi, and yakiniku supply shops
  - A retro space preserved in its Shōwa-era atmosphere
- **Korea Town (Korea Road)**: Motsu-yaki (offal grill), samgyeopsal, tteokbokki, hanbok, and more
  - Recently, thanks to the K-pop and Korean Wave boom, it has become a popular tourist destination for young Japanese as well
- Tsuruhashi's Zainichi Korean history offers a place to directly experience **the shadow side of modern Japanese history**

## 6. Himeji Castle (姫路城)

> Approximately 55 minutes from Osaka by JR Rapid Service — highly recommended as a day trip from Osaka

### History of the Castle
- **Early 14th century**: Akamatsu Sadanori (赤松貞範) first established a fortification on this site
- **1581**: Toyotomi Hideyoshi expanded it into a three-story keep during his Chūgoku campaign
- **1601–1609**: **Ikeda Terumasa (池田輝政)** undertook a massive expansion → the present form
  - He fought on the Tokugawa Eastern Army's side at the Battle of Sekigahara; appointed Lord of Harima as his reward
  - Completed western Japan's largest castle complex over 9 years; a western defensive stronghold of the Tokugawa
- No major battles afterward → preserved largely intact through the Edo, Meiji, and modern eras

### The Secret of the White Color — Shikkui Plaster (漆喰)
- The entire exterior walls are thickly coated with **shikkui (消石灰 + sand + seaweed mixed plaster)**
- **Purpose ①: Fire resistance** — defense against fire arrows and fire attacks; shikkui does not burn and actually hardens at high temperatures
- **Purpose ②: Moisture resistance** — prevents wood decay; extends structural lifespan
- **Purpose ③: Prestige** — the gleaming white exterior is visible from a great distance, displaying the lord's authority

### Shirasagi Castle (白鷺城, Egret Castle)
- The brilliant white exterior + the graceful curve of the roof resembling outstretched wings → likened to a white egret (shirasagi) spreading its wings
- During cherry blossom season, the combination of the white castle and pink blossoms creates an overwhelmingly beautiful landscape

### Architectural Features & UNESCO World Heritage
- **Renjū-shiki Tenshu (連立式天守, Compound Keep)**: The main keep and three smaller keeps connected by corridors — the pinnacle of Japanese castle architecture
- **Maze-like pathways within the castle**: Deliberately complex layout designed to disorient intruders
- **Gun ports (狭間, hazama)**: Triangular, circular, and square openings — for arrows, firearms, and projectiles respectively
- **UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 1993** (one of the first two sites inscribed in Japan)
- The largest and finest of the 12 surviving castle keeps; miraculously escaped damage in the Osaka air raids of 1945

|---|---|---|
