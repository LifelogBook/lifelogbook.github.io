# Vincent van Gogh — *Irises*

![Van Gogh Irises](/assets/images/getty/getty_vangogh_irises.jpg)

This is **Vincent van Gogh**'s 1889 masterpiece **\*Irises\***, one of the most beloved paintings in the world.

## Historical Background: "A Flower That Bloomed Through Pain"

This painting was created while van Gogh was a patient at the **Saint-Paul-de-Mausole** psychiatric asylum in Saint-Rémy, France.

- **Why he was admitted:** In December 1888, following a violent argument with fellow painter Paul Gauguin, van Gogh suffered a breakdown in which he cut off his own **left ear**. After treatment at a hospital in Arles, he voluntarily checked himself into this asylum in May 1889.
- **What the painting meant to him:** During his first week at the asylum, van Gogh discovered irises blooming in the hospital garden and began painting them. For him, the act of painting was the only shield against madness — **"a lightning rod against illness,"** as he described it.

## Artistic Features & Points of Appreciation

- **Vitality:** As noted on the museum label, van Gogh observed nature with extraordinary care. The contrast between the twisting, writhing green leaves and the intense violet petals fills the canvas with an overwhelming sense of life.
- **Influence of Japanese Art:** The bold black outlines around the flowers and the flat, close-up composition that ignores perspective reflect van Gogh's deep admiration for **Japanese woodblock prints (ukiyo-e)**.
- **The Lonely White Flower:** Look closely at the left side of the painting. Among the sea of purple irises, there is **a single white iris blooming alone**. Many critics interpret this as a self-portrait of van Gogh's own solitude.

## Reception in His Time

Van Gogh is often said to have died unrecognized, but *Irises* was an exception. His brother Theo marveled at it, saying it was "full of air and life." It was exhibited at the Salon des Indépendants in September 1889, where it received great admiration from fellow painters, especially Monet. This painting stands as proof that even in his darkest moment, van Gogh was desperately clinging to life.

# J.M.W. Turner — *Modern Rome – Campo Vaccino*

![Turner Modern Rome](/assets/images/getty/getty_turner.png)

This work is displayed on the second floor of the West Pavilion at the Getty Center and is considered one of the **most important and expensive landscape paintings** in the Getty's collection.

## Historical Background: "The Heart of Rome, Turned into a Cow Pasture"

The title **'Campo Vaccino'** is Italian for **'Cow Pasture.'**

- **Past glory:** The setting is the **Forum Romanum**, the political and religious center of the ancient Roman Empire. In the far left, you can make out the Colosseum, triumphal arches, and other ancient ruins.
- **19th-century reality:** But when Turner visited in 1839, this once-center of the world had been buried under soil, reduced to a meadow where cattle and goats grazed. Turner juxtaposed the crumbling ancient ruins (the past) with ordinary people and animals living among them (the present) to express the melancholy truth: **"No empire lasts forever."**

## Artistic Features: "The Magic of Light"

True to his nickname **'Painter of Light,'** Turner painted not the precise forms of buildings but the **light and air** that filled the Roman atmosphere.

- **Mood:** A soft, dreamlike golden haze of dusk (or early dawn) envelops the entire canvas, creating a mysteriously beautiful scene that feels like something from a dream.
- **Condition:** Because this painting was privately held by a British aristocratic family (the Earl of Rosebery) for much of its life, even after more than 180 years, the colors remain remarkably unfaded and nearly pristine.

## Getty's Triumph (A Staggering Price)

There is a famous story attached to this work. When it came up for auction at Sotheby's London in 2010, the Getty Museum bid over **£29.7 million (roughly $50 billion KRW at the time)** to win it. It was a record price for a Turner at the time, and the British public lamented that "a national treasure was being sold to America." It remains one of the most striking demonstrations of the Getty's extraordinary financial power.

# Édouard Manet — *Spring*

![Manet Spring](/assets/images/getty/getty_manet_spring.png)

One of the highlights of the Getty Center's West Pavilion: **Édouard Manet**'s 1881 masterpiece, **\*Spring\***.

A woman in a floral dress holds a parasol in this captivating painting — one that carries both Manet's final artistic spirit and a deeply poignant story.

## Historical Background: "An Unfinished Four Seasons Project"

In his later years, Manet planned a series of paintings representing the **Four Seasons** through fashion and feminine beauty.

- **The plan:** He intended to paint four works — Spring, Summer, Autumn, and Winter — capturing the beauty of Parisian women.
- **The tragedy:** Sadly, Manet's health was rapidly deteriorating due to illness (complications of syphilis). He died having completed only **\*Spring\*** and **\*Autumn\*** — making this one of his **final works and last masterpieces**.
- **The success:** A year before his death, in 1882, this painting was exhibited at the Paris Salon, where it received enormous praise from critics and the public alike. It was a sweet victory in the final chapter of a career spent at the center of controversy.

## The Model and the Fashion

- **The model:** The woman in the painting is **Jeanne de Marsy**, a celebrated Parisian actress and society figure at the time. Manet felt her haughty profile and refined air were perfect for expressing spring.
- **The fashion:** Look at her outfit — an elaborate floral dress, a bonnet decorated with flowers, beige gloves, and a parasol. This was the **height of Parisian fashion** in the 1880s. Manet painted her like a fashion magazine model, embodying the idea that **"spring is the season fashion begins."**

## Points of Appreciation

- **The background:** The foliage behind the woman is rendered in loose, blurred strokes — a hallmark of Impressionist technique, evoking the sensation of sunlight breaking through leaves.
- **The nose:** Manet deliberately painted the model's nose as upturned and slightly pert — emphasizing the typical charm of the spirited, coquettish Parisian woman (*Parisienne*) of the era.

## Getty's Record ($65 Million)

This painting is also a landmark event in Getty Museum history. In 2014, the Getty acquired it at Christie's New York for over **$65 million (approximately 70 billion KRW at the time)** — a record price for any Manet at the time. It speaks volumes about just how important the Getty considers this work.

Experience the radiance of this painting, made by Manet just before his death as a hymn to the most beautifully blooming season of all.

# Claude Monet — *The Portal of Rouen Cathedral in Morning Light*

![Monet Rouen Cathedral](/assets/images/getty/getty_monet_cathedral.png)

This is **Claude Monet**'s **\*The Portal of Rouen Cathedral in Morning Light\*** (1894), a masterpiece of Impressionism.

## What Is It Depicting?

The painting shows the main façade (portal) of the great cathedral in Rouen, France. But for Monet, what mattered was not the **building** itself.

## Why This Painting Is Famous (The Series)

As noted on the museum label, Monet rented a room across the street from the cathedral and painted **the same view more than 30 times**.

- "Why would he keep painting the same thing?"
  - **Light.** Monet wanted to demonstrate that as light changes with the time of day and weather, the color and feel of an object transforms completely.

## What Makes This Version Unique (Morning)

- **Color:** True to its title, **\*Morning Light\***, the palette is dominated by **cool bluish-grey tones**. Can you feel the mysterious, mist-laden air of a hazy dawn? (The same subject painted at dusk is filled with reds and golds.)
- **Texture:** Up close, the paint is applied in thick, uneven layers. Rather than defining the hard lines of a building, Monet captured the shimmering *atmosphere* of the cathedral as it was struck by light.

Have you looked at this painting up close? From inches away, it looks like an indecipherable mass of paint — but step back, and a magnificent cathedral appears. That is the magic of Monet.

# Workshop of Leonardo da Vinci — *Virgin and Child with Saint Anne in a Landscape* (1508–13)

![Da Vinci Workshop](/assets/images/getty/getty_davinci.png)

This painting is a copy of Leonardo da Vinci's masterpiece **\*The Virgin and Child with Saint Anne\*** — made by one of his pupils.

## Who Made It? (Leonardo's 'Workshop')

The painting was not made by Leonardo himself, but it was produced in his **workshop (bottega)**.

- **The likely artist:** Scholars believe the painter was **Salaì** (mentioned on the museum label), whose full name was Gian Giacomo Caprotti.
- **Their relationship:** Salaì entered da Vinci's workshop at age 10 and lived alongside him for approximately **25 years** — the longest-serving pupil and companion in da Vinci's life. Leonardo was deeply fond of him, and Salaì developed an extraordinary ability to reproduce his master's works.

## Historical Background: The Original at the Louvre

Do you see the small photograph attached to the bottom-left of the museum label? That is da Vinci's original, now housed in the **Louvre Museum** in Paris.

- **How it was made:** In the workshops of famous Renaissance painters, once the master completed an original, pupils would copy it as training exercises or to create saleable replicas.
- **Its value:** This work was likely painted under da Vinci's direct supervision. As a faithful and high-quality contemporary reproduction — capturing the original's composition and mysterious sfumato technique — it holds enormous historical significance.

## Symbolism and Meaning

What looks like a peaceful family portrait contains hints of tragic destiny.

- **The Christ Child and the lamb:** The infant Jesus is **pulling a lamb's ears** in play. The lamb symbolizes the **sacrifice** Jesus will make for humanity on the cross.
- **The Virgin Mary (the mother):** Mary reaches out to pull the child back toward her, as if trying to keep him from the lamb (his fate). She embodies a mother's human love and anxiety.
- **Saint Anne (the grandmother):** Seated at the back, Mary's mother looks upon the scene with a calm, gentle gaze. She represents wisdom — accepting the destiny that awaits her grandson.

**In short:** This is a work in which da Vinci's most beloved pupil, Salaì, reproduced his master's masterpiece with near-perfect fidelity — a precious document of Renaissance artistic tradition and pedagogy.

# Pontormo — *Portrait of a Halberdier*

![Pontormo Portrait of a Halberdier](/assets/images/getty/getty_pontormo.png)

This is **Pontormo**'s **\*Portrait of a Halberdier\*** — so celebrated and beautiful it has been called **"the Mona Lisa of the Getty Center."**

## Who on Earth Is He?

The identity of this young man has been one of art history's great debates for centuries. There are two leading candidates, and the historical meaning of the painting changes entirely depending on who it is.

- **Candidate 1: Francesco Guardi (Defender of the Republic)**
  - **Context:** In 1529, Florence was under siege by the Holy Roman Empire (the **Siege of Florence**). Young noble boys also took up arms to defend the Florentine Republic.
  - **Interpretation:** Under this theory, the painting depicts a **patriotic boy-soldier** steeling himself to defend his country. The **halberd** he carries and the sword at his hip symbolize his resolve to protect the Republic.
- **Candidate 2: Cosimo I de' Medici (The Future Ruler)**
  - **Context:** The alternative theory holds that this is a young **Cosimo I**, who would seize power in Florence in 1537 and become Grand Duke of Tuscany.
  - **Interpretation:** In this case, the painting is a portrait asserting the **authority of a young ruler** who has defeated his rivals. The confident pose and luxurious clothing signal aristocratic power.
  - **Interesting detail:** Recent research analyzing the shape of the boy's fingers has lent support to the Cosimo I theory — but it remains unconfirmed.

The debate has been especially fierce for three reasons:

### 1. Conflicting Records (Was It Vasari's Fault?)
**Giorgio Vasari**, the father of Renaissance art history, left an ambiguous account in his writings.
- Vasari recorded that Pontormo painted **both** "a portrait of Francesco Guardi" **and** "a portrait of Cosimo de' Medici."
- One of those two paintings has since been lost. The one before us — *Portrait of a Halberdier* — is the only one that survives. Hence the dispute: is this the Guardi, or the Cosimo?

### 2. Political Stakes (Hero vs. Tyrant)
The answer matters because it completely **changes the story** the painting tells.
- **If Guardi:** "A pure, patriotic boy defending his homeland from invasion." (A symbol of democracy and the Republic.)
- **If Cosimo:** "The young future ruler of Florence." (A symbol of Medici power and dominance.)

In other words, depending on who the subject is, the figure becomes either a **freedom fighter** or an **embodiment of autocracy** — which is precisely why scholars have refused to concede.

### 3. "Too Beautiful to Pin Down"
Pontormo painted exquisitely — but **idealistically** rather than realistically.
- Pontormo's Mannerist style tends to render subjects pale and elegantly beautified. He painted the boy more like an **impossibly beautiful youth** than a specific individual, making it harder to match the image to any known facial characteristics.

Ultimately, this is a delightful controversy born from the fact that **"it's so famous because of its genius, yet the records are vague and the face is too beautiful to be certain."**

## Artistic Features: "The Contradiction of War and Youth"

True to his mastery of **Mannerism**, Pontormo expressed subtle and sophisticated emotion.

- **Dual appeal:** The boy holds a fearsome weapon (a halberd), yet his face is barely past the age of soft, downy hair. The contrast between the **brutal instrument of war** and the **fragile beauty of youth** creates a strange, compelling tension.
- **Fashion:** The red cap and trousers and puffed sleeves were the **latest fashion** among Florentine upper-class society of the day.

## Getty's Treasure

When the Getty Museum purchased this painting in 1989, it set a **world record price for an Old Master painting (approximately $35 million)**. A testament to its immense art-historical value — and one of the Getty's most prized possessions.

**Viewing tip:** Look carefully at the boy's eyes. Is it fear before the battlefield — or the arrogance of a future ruler? That ambiguity is the painting's greatest power.

# Cauldron with a Satyr

![Cauldron with a Satyr](/assets/images/getty/getty_cauldron.png)

This curious object is a **\*Cauldron with a Satyr\*** made in Greece in the 1st century BC (approximately 2,100 years ago). It offers a fascinating glimpse into the **luxurious party culture of the ancient elite**.

## The Star: A Drunken Satyr

The figure bursting from the top of the cauldron is a **Satyr**.

- **Identity:** A half-human, half-beast spirit that follows Dionysus, the god of wine — a symbol of instinct and pleasure.
- **Pose:** He holds a wine cup in his left hand, his expression gloriously intoxicated and exuberant — as if he's shouting at the party guests, "Come on, one more round!"
- **Detail:** According to the museum label, the Satyr's **eyes and teeth** are inlaid with **silver**. In ancient lamplight, they would have gleamed brilliantly, making the figure seem startlingly alive.

## Function: "A Mobile Wine Trolley"

This large vessel is a **lebes** — a bowl for mixing wine with water. (Ancient Greeks considered undiluted wine too strong to drink straight.)

- **The wheels:** The most delightful feature is the **four small wheels** on the base.
- **Why:** When full of wine, the vessel would be extremely heavy. The wheels allowed it to be **rolled to wherever guests were gathered** to serve them — essentially the ancient equivalent of a **wheeled cooler** or a **mobile cocktail bar**.

## Lavish Decoration

Along the body below the Satyr, intricate vine and floral patterns are finely carved — with silver also used in the floral decoration.

**In short:** This is a brilliantly inventive piece of art that reveals how much the wealthy of ancient Greece cared about making their parties not just enjoyable, but spectacular.

# The Mazarin Venus

![Mazarin Venus](/assets/images/getty/getty_venus.png)

This sculpture is one of the Getty Center's most iconic holdings: **\*The Mazarin Venus\***.

## Who Does It Depict?

It depicts **Venus (Aphrodite)**, the goddess of love and beauty.

- **The dolphin:** Look at the left side of the goddess's feet — there is a **dolphin**. According to myth, Venus was born from the sea foam, and in ancient art the dolphin is an important emblem of her oceanic origins.
- **The pose:** Her unclothed figure, holding a drape, depicts her either preparing for or having just completed a bath.

## Historical Background: "Copied by Rome, Beloved by France"

- **When it was made:** This sculpture was created in the **2nd century AD** (approximately 100–200 CE), during the Roman era. It is not an entirely original creation, however — it is a Roman copy of a celebrated Greek Venus statue from centuries earlier.
- **The name (Mazarin):** Why "Mazarin"? Because in the 17th century, the legendary French statesman **Cardinal Mazarin** owned this sculpture. He was the mentor of Louis XIV, the Sun King, and the most powerful man of his age.

## Points of Appreciation

Having passed through many owners over the centuries, some parts of the sculpture (such as the head or arms) are believed to have been restored or added in later periods. Even so, this remains a fine work that beautifully preserves the graceful proportions and beauty of an ancient goddess.

# A Golden Tablet — A "Guidebook to the Underworld" and a "Ticket to Paradise"

![Golden Tablet](/assets/images/getty/getty_goldentablet.png)

This small golden object was made in ancient Greece around 350 BC (approximately 2,300 years ago). It functioned as a kind of **guidebook to the underworld** — a **"ticket to paradise"** for the dead.

## What Was It Used For? (A Cheat Sheet for the Deceased)

Followers of ancient Greek mystery religions — **Orphism** or Dionysian cults — believed that upon death, the soul would be judged in the underworld (Hades).

This golden tablet was buried with a believer at death. Engraved on it is a **script** that the soul was meant to memorize — the words needed to find the right path in the underworld and reach a place of bliss (paradise).

## What It Says (A Dialogue with the Gatekeeper)

In tiny script, the tablet records the conversation the soul must have with the spring (guardian) of the underworld. (Based on the English translation on the museum label.)

- **The Soul (the deceased):** "I am parched with thirst and perishing!"
- **The Spring (guardian):** "Come here and drink from me. I am the ever-flowing spring… But who are you? Where do you come from?"
- **The Soul (the correct answer):** **"I am a child of Earth and of starry Heaven. My race is of Heaven."**

**The key point:** The deceased must prove that they are not merely human, but a being of **divine origin ("a child of Heaven")** — only then can they avoid drinking from the River Lethe (the river of forgetfulness) and instead be granted eternal life in paradise.

## Why Was It Made of Gold?

The reason it was engraved in **gold** rather than written on papyrus is that gold does not rust or decay — it shines forever. This symbolizes the **immortality of the soul**.

**In short:** This small object is the **"passport to the afterlife"** that someone 2,300 years ago clutched in their hand as they faced death — determined not to be afraid, but to enter the realm of the gods as a divine child, standing tall.
